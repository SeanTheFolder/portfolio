In Depth

Perspectives on Healthcare Security

Why Microsoft Is the Primary Ecosystem

Consolidating onto Microsoft was strategic, not dogmatic. Sentinel, Defender, Purview, and Intune create a tightly integrated environment where data flows between tools without manual correlation. In a lean org, that integration multiplies what one person can monitor, respond to, and report on. Best-of-breed still wins when the gap justifies the complexity. WatchGuard, Ubiquiti, Aruba, and Tenable are all in production because they were the right tools for those jobs.

AI in Healthcare Security: Practical, Not Theoretical

AI adoption in healthcare carries real risk. Data governance, prompt injection, and oversharing exposure are all live concerns. That's exactly why the CISO should lead the AI conversation, not react to it. I deploy Copilot Studio agents with the same governance rigor I apply to any system: Purview DLP on prompts, Conditional Access on agent surfaces, audit logging through Sentinel. Security Copilot handles alert triage and KQL generation. Custom agents handle service desk routing and QA documentation. Each one is governed, auditable, and operates within HIPAA boundaries.

Digital Transformation Is Security Work

Implementing HubSpot CRM and CMS, rebuilding a decade-old website on Webflow, and standing up ADP were not diversions from security. Every paper-based process is a data governance risk. Every unmaintained site is an attack surface. Digital transformation reduces attack surface, improves auditability, and replaces brittle processes with monitored systems.

Physical Security Is Part of the Security Program

Physical and digital security are not separate disciplines. They're the same discipline with different attack surfaces. Tailgating is credential theft. An access control system that doesn't integrate with identity management is a gap. Camera systems running on unmanaged firmware are unmonitored endpoints. When I deployed DMP Entré and the Ubiquiti camera infrastructure, I treated it identically to any other system: documented, enrolled, governed, and audited. Physical access events route to the same audit trail as logical access events. A security program that secures the cloud but leaves the server room door open isn't a security program.

The Healthcare Regulatory Stack Is a Design Input

Healthcare security isn't generic security with a compliance layer bolted on. The regulatory stack is layered, overlapping, and actively enforced. HIPAA Security Rule. HITECH breach notification. 42 CFR Part 418 hospice Conditions of Participation. OIG compliance guidance for home health agencies and hospices. The Information Blocking Rule. As of February 2026, a new 42 CFR Part 2 enforcement deadline for SUD record confidentiality. OCR's third HIPAA audit phase is currently underway, targeting risk analysis specifically. The False Claims Act creates legal liability when billing systems are compromised. In that environment, compliance isn't a project you run once a year. It's an architectural principle that shapes every system, every access control policy, every audit log, and every vendor contract. I treat the regulatory stack as a design input, not a checklist. When auditors arrive, there's nothing to scramble for.

Executive Conversations

Questions I Get Asked

You rose from IT Specialist to CISO in three years at the same organization. How do we know that's merit and not title inflation?+

The answer is in the implementations. When I arrived, there was no SIEM, no MDM, no documented security posture, no compliance program, no physical security infrastructure worth the name. Everything in the Implementations section was built during that window. By me, hands-on, while simultaneously running IT operations. Each promotion followed a specific, demonstrable expansion of scope: Analyst when the security program had legs, Director when technology strategy needed executive ownership, CISO when I was already operating at that scope. The title came after the work, not before it.

What does the healthcare compliance landscape look like for a home health and hospice CISO?+

It's layered and specific. You're operating under the HIPAA Security and Privacy Rules as the baseline, HITECH for breach notification obligations, and OIG compliance program guidance for both home health agencies and hospices. Those are two separate sets of fraud and abuse risk areas. 42 CFR Part 418 Medicare Conditions of Participation govern clinical record-keeping requirements, so IT systems have to support clinical documentation integrity. As of February 2026, the 42 CFR Part 2 final rule added new obligations for organizations handling substance use disorder records, actively enforced by OCR. The Information Blocking Rule creates civil penalty exposure if health IT systems interfere with data access or exchange. The False Claims Act creates direct legal liability when billing systems are compromised. HITRUST CSF is the industry framework that ties all of this together into a single certifiable program. Our security architecture is built toward that standard. An IT leader who doesn't know what OIG CPG says about cybersecurity, or what 42 CFR Part 418 requires for clinical records, is operating blind in this environment.

How do you present security risk to a board without a technical background?+

I don't lead with CVEs or patch counts. Every significant risk gets translated into one of three business terms: operational continuity, regulatory exposure, or reputational cost. A critical vulnerability in a patient-facing system isn't a CVSS 9.8. It's a potential breach notification event, a six-figure remediation cost, and an OCR investigation. When the board understands inaction in those terms, funding decisions get easier. I maintain a standing one-page risk dashboard. Top risks mapped to business impact, updated quarterly, never more than one page. The board should be making decisions, not reading reports.

Your career has been largely in one sector and one organization. Is that a limitation?+

I'd reframe it. I built a mature security program from nothing, in a regulated environment, on a nonprofit budget, without a breach. That's harder than inheriting a program. On breadth, my consulting years covered commercial, hospitality, retail, and creative industries across infrastructure, physical security, and development work. Different threat models, different operational cultures. The depth at Harbors is proof of sustained execution. The early years are proof of adaptability. I'm not a one-trick operator.

What's your approach to AI governance in a HIPAA-regulated environment?+

The CISO needs to lead the AI conversation, not react to it. I've deployed Copilot Studio agents with the same governance rigor as any other system: Purview DLP on prompt inputs, Conditional Access scoping agent surfaces, audit logging through Sentinel. Every agent has a defined data boundary. Shadow AI, meaning employees using unsanctioned LLM tools, gets treated as a DLP and data governance problem, not a policy conversation. We block what we haven't approved and fast-track approval for what has legitimate business use. Security Copilot is in production for alert triage and KQL generation. The governance model isn't a constraint on the AI strategy. It's the prerequisite for it.

How do you balance being technically hands-on while operating at the executive level?+

In a lean org, you don't get to choose. And I wouldn't want to. The hands-on context makes me a better executive because I can't be sold vaporware, and my technical staff can't bury problems in jargon. I write KQL. I've configured WatchGuard policies. I've physically run cable. That said, I'm deliberate about which technical work I own versus delegate. I stay close to architecture decisions, detection logic, and anything touching HIPAA data flows. I step back from repeatable operational work that can be documented and owned by others. The line isn't "strategic vs. technical." It's "high-judgment vs. repeatable."

What does your first 90 days look like in a new organization?+

First 30 days: listen and assess. Understand what the business does, who the real decision-makers are, what the risk posture looks like on paper versus in reality, and where the regulatory exposure sits. No changes. Days 30–60: produce a written risk assessment and a prioritized remediation roadmap framed in business impact terms. Present it. Get alignment before building. Days 60–90: begin executing on the highest-priority items with visible, measurable outcomes. By day 90, the organization should have one tangible security improvement it didn't have before, and leadership should have a clear picture of where we're going and why.

Full-time, consulting, or both?+

Both, depending on fit. For full-time, I'm looking for organizations where security is a genuine strategic priority, not a compliance checkbox. For consulting, I work best with organizations that need to build or mature a program, navigate a specific compliance challenge, or want experienced hands on a high-stakes implementation. I don't do security theater on either side.
